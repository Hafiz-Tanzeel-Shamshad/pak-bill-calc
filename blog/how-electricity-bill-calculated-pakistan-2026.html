How is Electricity Bill Calculated in Pakistan? Complete NEPRA 2026 Guide

By Hafiz Tanzeel Shamshad — Updated: May 20, 2026

Every month, millions of Pakistani households receive an electricity bill that looks complicated at first glance. There are slabs, taxes, and several surcharges. The good news is that the calculation follows a predictable pattern. Once you understand the steps, you can estimate your bill before it arrives and avoid surprises. This guide explains the NEPRA 2026 structure in plain English and shows where each charge comes from.

At a high level, your bill has three layers. First are the energy charges based on slab rates. Next are fixed charges and taxes like GST and income tax. Finally, there are adjustment charges such as FCA. Each layer adds to the total. To estimate quickly, you can use the Pakistan electricity bill calculator and then compare the breakdown with your official bill.

The same formula applies across DISCOs like LESCO, MEPCO, IESCO, and others. While local fees can vary slightly, the NEPRA base tariff is the standard reference for 2026. That is why a single calculator can serve all major regions. If you want a focused estimate, choose a DISCO specific calculator such as LESCO.

Step 1: Identify your consumer type

NEPRA divides residential users into lifeline, protected, and non-protected consumers. Lifeline users are extremely low consumption households, often below 50 units. Protected consumers have an average of 200 units or less over the last three months. Non-protected consumers are above that threshold. Your consumer type determines which slab rates apply to your units. If you are unsure, check your last three bills or use the guidance in our protected consumer explanation.

Commercial users often follow a different rate structure. For simplicity, most household calculators treat commercial as a non-protected slab, which is close to the base tariff for estimate purposes. If you have a commercial meter, your bill may include additional charges from your DISCO.

Step 2: Apply the slab rates to units

Once you know your consumer type, your units are divided into slabs. Each slab has its own per-unit price. For example, a non-protected household might pay a lower rate for the first 100 units and a higher rate for the next 100 units. This is why the total can jump when your usage crosses a slab boundary. The full slab chart for 2026 is listed in our tariff slab guide.

Think of it like a staircase. Each step is a slab. When you move to the next step, all additional units are charged at a higher price. This is the most important reason to track your monthly usage, especially during peak summer months when AC usage rises sharply.

Step 3: Add fixed charges based on load

Most households have a sanctioned load below 5 kW. In that case, the minimum monthly bill is Rs 600. If your sanctioned load is 5 kW or higher, the minimum bill is Rs 2,000. Many people overlook this, but it means even low usage months can have a meaningful bill. The fixed charge is applied after energy charges and before some surcharges.

Step 4: Apply GST and income tax

GST is applied as a percentage of your energy charges. In 2026, the standard GST rate is 18 percent. Income tax is also applied to many domestic bills, and the rate depends on your filer status. Filers pay a lower rate than non-filers. This is why two households with the same units can still have different totals. To learn more, see our income tax guide.

Step 5: Add surcharges and adjustments

NEPRA approved surcharges include the Neelum Jhelum surcharge and the financing cost surcharge. These are usually small per-unit amounts, but they add up for higher usage. There is also a TV license fee and a small meter rent amount. The last major item is the Fuel Cost Adjustment (FCA). FCA is the most variable line item because it changes monthly based on fuel prices and generation mix. Some months it is positive; other months it is negative, which reduces your bill. You can learn more about FCA in our Fuel Cost Adjustment guide.

When you put all of these pieces together, you get the total estimate. A clear breakdown helps you understand where the money goes and which factors you can control. Unit usage and slab position are the biggest controllable drivers. Taxes and surcharges are mostly fixed by regulation.

Example: Estimating a 300-unit bill

Suppose you consumed 300 units and you are a non-protected consumer. The first 100 units are charged at the first slab rate, the next 100 at a higher rate, and the final 100 at a higher rate again. Add the fixed charge based on load, then add GST and income tax. Finally, add per-unit surcharges and FCA if you choose to include it. This is exactly what the main calculator does for you, and it also provides a slab table and visual chart.

Why your official bill can differ

Even a careful estimate can differ from your official bill. The main reasons are monthly FCA changes, quarterly tariff adjustments, meter reading differences, or late payment charges. This is why it is best to use an estimate as a planning tool rather than a final statement. If you see a large difference, compare the unit reading and ask your DISCO for clarification.

FAQs

What is a slab in an electricity bill?

A slab is a range of units that has its own per-unit rate. The rate increases as you consume more units.

Who is a protected consumer in Pakistan?

Protected consumers are households with an average of 200 units or less over the last three months.

Is GST applied to the whole bill?

GST is generally applied on energy charges before other surcharges are added.

Why does FCA change every month?

FCA depends on fuel prices and generation costs, which change monthly.
