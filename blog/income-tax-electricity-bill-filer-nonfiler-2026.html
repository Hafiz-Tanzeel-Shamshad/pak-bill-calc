Income Tax on Electricity Bill in Pakistan - Filer vs Non-Filer 2026

By Hafiz Tanzeel Shamshad — Updated: May 20, 2026

Many Pakistani consumers are surprised to see income tax on their electricity bills. This tax is collected as withholding tax under rules like Section 235 and is applied to energy charges. The rate is lower for filers and higher for non-filers, which is why two households with the same units can have different totals.

To estimate the impact, use the main bill calculator and switch between filer and non-filer status. The difference can be significant over a year.

How the tax is applied

The income tax is typically applied to energy charges, not the full bill. This means the higher your units and slab rate, the higher the tax amount. If your household is in a high slab, the tax impact becomes more noticeable. The tax is collected under Section 235 of the Income Tax Ordinance, which allows DISCOs to collect withholding tax at the time of billing. This is why you see a separate line item for income tax on your monthly bill.

It is important to understand that this is not a new tax. It has been part of Pakistan electricity billing for several years. However, the rate changes based on your taxpayer status. If you switch from non-filer to filer, the rate drops significantly. The difference can be hundreds of rupees every month for a typical household in the 300 to 500 unit range.

Filer vs non-filer rates in 2026

Filers pay about 7.5 percent withholding tax on energy charges. Non-filers pay about 15 percent, which is double. This means a household using 400 units as a non-filer could be paying over Rs 1,500 in income tax alone. The same household as a filer would pay around half that amount.

If you are unsure whether you are a filer, check your recent tax return or ask your tax consultant. Many households are registered filers without knowing it because their employer deducts tax. Even if you file a simple nil return each year, you are treated as a filer for electricity bill tax purposes. This can reduce your monthly bill noticeably.

Example: tax difference for a typical household

Suppose your energy charges for the month are Rs 12,000. As a filer, you would pay approximately Rs 900 in income tax. As a non-filer, you would pay around Rs 1,800. The difference of Rs 900 per month adds up to Rs 10,800 over a year. For higher usage households, the gap is even wider. This is why becoming a filer is one of the fastest ways to reduce your bill without changing your appliance usage.

You can verify this difference using the main bill calculator. Enter your units and switch between filer and non-filer to see the exact difference for your situation. The calculator also shows how GST and surcharges compare side by side.

How to reduce the tax impact

The best way is to become a filer. This lowers the rate applied to your energy charges. You can also reduce the tax amount by reducing total units, staying under lower slabs, and avoiding high seasonal usage. If you are already a filer but still see high tax, check whether your energy charges have increased due to a slab jump. Moving from 200 to 300 units can double the tax amount even at the same filer rate.

For non-filers, the most effective step is to file your income tax return. The process is straightforward and can be done online through the IRIS portal. You do not need to have a large income to file. Even a nil return establishes your filer status and lowers your electricity tax rate. If you are unsure, consult a tax professional or visit your nearest tax facilitation center for help.

Why the bill can still differ between months

Even with the same units and same filer status, your bill can differ because of FCA changes, surcharges, or arrears. The FCA adjustment changes every month and is added to energy charges before tax is calculated. This means your income tax amount can go up or down even if your units stayed the same. That is why it helps to check the FCA line on your bill each month to understand the change.

Use the calculator on this site to see how each line item changes your total. The bill calculation guide explains each step in detail and shows how the tax amount flows from the tariff slab system. If you are comparing two bills, check the FCA and unit count first before assuming the tax rate changed.

Section 235 and the legal framework

Section 235 of the Income Tax Ordinance covers withholding tax on electricity bills. It applies to all residential and commercial consumers above a minimum threshold. The DISCO collects the tax and deposits it with the government on your behalf. This is similar to how your employer deducts tax from your salary. You can claim credit for this tax when you file your annual return. If too much was deducted, you may get a refund after filing.

If you believe the tax has been incorrectly applied, contact your DISCO customer service first. They can check whether your filer status was recorded correctly in their system. If the issue continues, file a complaint with the tax authority or approach a tax professional. Keep your past bills organized so you can compare the tax line over several months and spot anomalies.
